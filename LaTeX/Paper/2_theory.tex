\section*{Political Violence \& Civilian Populations: An Inclusive Model of Conflict Evolution}

More often than not, stories of armed conflict, war, or widespread criminal violence shine a spotlight on actors who seek to wield power through collective violence against a given population. Recently, however, this narrative has begun to change. With uprisings across the last 5 years--the Tunisian revolution, the Egyptian revolution, the Syrian Civil War, and the 2014 protests in Venezuela--attention has shifted to recognize the potential power of the population to influence environments of extreme violence and repression. 

Compared to the number of studies focusing on economic \citep{collier:hoeffler:2004}, political \citep{humphreys:weinstein:2008}, and identity-based \citep{cederman:etal:2010a} drivers of conflict intensity and duration, existing research has largely neglected the role of civilians in influencing the behavior of armed actors. A large body of research has explored the origins of collective action and mobilization \citep{gurr:1970, opp:1988, tarrow:1994, tucker:2007}, and recent scholarship has assessed the effectiveness of ``maximalist campaigns" to show that countries are more likely to be democratic following nonviolent campaigns \citep{chenoweth:stephan:2011}. However, as articulated by \citet{celestino:gleditsch:2013}, even the macro-level relationship between nonviolent campaigns and state-level outcomes such as democracy or regime transitions remains unclear. Importantly, \citeauthor{celestino:gleditsch:2013} show that nonviolent campaigns destabilize regimes, but that the trajectory of peace and democracy following such campaigns is conditional on the precise actions employed by campaign organizers. 

\subsection*{Protests \& Armed Conflict}

Building on the research agenda motivated by macro-level studies, we investigate the link between nonviolent action and violence at the local level. There is little empirical evidence that protests should or should not ``work" in contexts of high violence at the sub-state level. There are several possible logics linking protest and violence between armed groups.

First, there might be no relationship between protests and violence. Protests might have other benefits and thus persist for reasons outside of the stated goals driving protest organizers. For example, non-violent activism via protests can stimulate community between survivors of violence and foster a sense of purpose and belonging in the midst of crisis. Additionally, protests against insecurity and issues of violence might be targeted at both types of armed actors (i.e., the state and the non-state challenger), these actors could respond to protests through other means outside of violent strategy. Non-state armed groups might make public appeals to civilians, saying that they are there for civilians' protection. Similarly, governments can respond with media campaigns, speeches, or through a general effort to try to divert political attention from violent events. A second mechanism driving a null relationship between protests and violent events is the high level of risk for protesters. Following a protest, participants might be targeted because of their activism, possibly resulting in fewer protests over time but with no clear consequences for violence between armed groups.

Second, protest participants might achieve their stated aims and decrease violent conflicts in their region. If protests successfully demand that the government invest greater resources in the prevention of violence, then we would expect fewer incidents of violence following protests. If protests also demand that non-state actors stop warfare with the state, this manifests as a public disapproval of attempts by the non-state group to coerce or control the civilian population. With enough discontent, protests disrupt the ability of non-state actors to operate in their region.

Third, protests could lead to an increase in violence. As violence between armed actors (state and non-state) increases, non-state actors often victimize civilians to highlight the government's inability to protect the population. Even if protestors demand a non-violent solution to warfare, a plausible logic connecting protests and violence is that protests raise the cost of government inaction so that government armed actors are more likely to visibly fight hard in the next round of contestation. This logic would suggest an antagonistic government actor, one who is politically motivated to demonstrate competency through violent policy. 

In addition to civilian mobilization, we examine how civilian victimization influences conflict dynamics. High levels of civilian victimization could constrain the ability of armed actors' to fight: civilian communities, once heavily targeted, are potentially less likely to provide resources for rebel groups, such as food, shelter, or information about opponents. According to \cite{humphreys:weinstein:2006} extraction (or in some cases voluntary support) from civilians becomes abusive when it is so extreme that it removes a civilian's ability to survive. Such violent victimization means that civilians can no longer ``produce" the kinds of goods and resources vital to violent movements. We might also expect these constrained groups to be targeted by opportunistic opponents. Targeting civilians might also provoke violent responses for the sake of revenge, as \citet{condra:shapiro:2012} find when examining coalition targeting of civilians in Iraq. Conversely, \citet{lyall:2009}, find that when civilians from villages in Chechnya were targeted, this decreased the likelihood of retaliation.

Thus, we have significant scholarly disagreement about the relationship between civilian victimization and conflict. One problem here is endogeneity between the type of groups that might target civilians (thought to be both weaker and more violent) and general levels of violence. Our network approach is useful here because of the ability to account for both the first-order correlates of civilian victimization (which groups are likely to target civilians) and the interdependent, strategic effects of actually targeting civilians. In this paper, we test these competing logics using the case of Nigeria.


\subsection*{Network Based Approach}

Our second key contribution is modeling intrastate conflict evolution via a network formation perspective. In the field of conflict studies more generally, network approaches are gaining more attention \citep{kinne:2012,metternich:etal:2015,minhas:etal:2016,gallop:2017}. The study of intrastate conflict, however, has fallen behind in the application of these tools. The primary motivation for a network based approach is to account for the possibility that the actions of actors in a system are interdependent. The benefits of analyzing a system of social interactions as a system, rather than as independent dyadic relationships, is that researchers are able to better account for the underlying process that generates relational data. This results in less biased parameter estimates, better calibrated confidence intervals and, accordingly, the ability to make better predictions \citep{snijders:1996,hoff:ward:2004,cranmer:desmarais:2011,erikson:pinto:2014}. 

Social systems are complex; networks allow us to embrace these complexities rather than ignore them. The types of dependencies that manifest in networks can be grouped into three orders of dependencies. In social systems representing conflict or violence, some actors will ``send'' or may ``receive'' more violence than other actors in the network: this is termed first-order dependency. Additionally actors that are receivers of violence might respond in kind. These kind of \textit{reciprocal} interactions are intuitive to the study of civil conflict and are known as second-order dependencies of a network%, a concept easily captured by our approach
. 

Similarly, we examine other endogenous developments of a network such as the concept of \textit{stochastic equivalence} and \textit{homophily} \citep{wasserman:faust:1994}. Stochastic equivalence may manifest in a network in which actors cluster into groups, and the patterns of relations that a given actor has are determined by the group they fall into. More specifically, a pair of actors $ij$ are stochastically equivalent if the probability of $i$ relating to, and being related to, by every other actor is the same as the probability for $j$ \citep{anderson:etal:1992}. Homophily represents the idea that some armed actors might fight with one another due to a given set of shared characteristics, such as ideology, or region. Homophily can be used to explain the emergence of patterns such as transitivity (``a friend of a friend is a friend'') and balance (``an enemy of a friend is an enemy'') \citep{shalizi:thomas:2011}. The presence of these types of patterns within relational data structures is why we often find third-order dependencies within relational data structures. 

The principal idea behind employing a network based approach is that the actions, or inactions, of any actor are taking place within an interdependent system. Given this the entrance or even exit of an actor may have a notable impact on the type of conflict patterns we see across the network. Within the Nigerian case that we discuss next, we will show that the entrance of Boko Haram into the Nigerian conflict network had a notable impact on the stability of parts of the system in which they were not even directly involved.
