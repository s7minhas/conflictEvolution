\section*{Introduction}

Since 1946 more than half of the countries in the international system have experienced a civil war \citep{gleditsch:etal:2002}, and although wars between countries have waned in recent years, there are a growing number of intrastate conflicts \citep{pettersson:wallensteen:2015}. These types of conflicts have proven to be costly, as battles between state and non-state actors within countries as diverse as the Ukraine, Syria, and Nigeria has led to some of the highest yearly fatality counts in the post-Cold War period. The prevalence and costliness of such conflicts have resulted in the development of a rich literature on the causes and consequences of civil war.\footnote{For example, see \citet{hegre:etal:2001,collier:etal:2004,salehyan:2008,lacina:2014,minhas:radford:2016}.} 

 The intrastate conflict literature has primarily rooted itself in two modes of analysis: the country-year research design and government vs. non-state actor dyadic research designs. The country-year design is typically used to understand why some states devolve into civil conflict and others remain immune. The government-rebel group dyadic design \citep{cunningham:etal:2009} considers how attributes of non-state actors as well as their relationship to the state can be used to understand conflicts. Correspondingly, these research agendas have promoted substantial growth in data on battle-deaths \citep{lacina:2006, gleditsch:etal:2002}; battle location \citep{raleigh:etal:2010}; attributes of armed actors associated with combat events \citep{raleigh:etal:2010}, and civilian victimization as related to war-time processes \citep{eck:hultman:2007}. This body of literature has fostered further investigation not just into how conflicts begin, but also how they evolve and end. 
 
 These empirical perspectives, however, have yet to provide full consideration of the interconnected multi-actor dynamics that characterize recent examples of intrastate conflicts; this is a notable absence given that by 2003 over 30\% of ongoing civil wars involved multiple dyads in conflict with one another \citep{harbom:etal:2008}. For example, in the current Syrian civil war, Bashar al-Assad's government is fighting against the Islamic State and multiple militarized opposition groups (e.g., the Free Syrian Army, Jaysh al-Islam, Ahrar al-Sham and others). Each of these actors is often involved in battles both with the government and amongst themselves. In other regions, like Mexico, we observe drug trafficking organizations (DTOs) vying both for political influence and territorial control against government actors and other DTOs.  Complex systems of conflict dynamics such as those can be found in countries as varied as India, Colombia, and Nigeria. Many researchers recognize that such cases require a consideration of how non-state groups relate to one another and the government \citep{fotini:2012,cunningham:2014}. Empirical research on intrastate conflict, however, has done little to recognize that the intrastate conflicts many countries are embroiled in are understood best as a single complex system composed of multiple actors in conflict with one another rather than as a set of independent, dyadic conflicts.

Systems of this sort have long been studied in other fields such as sociology, where researchers have argued that to understand an actor's (or group's) behavior it is necessary to understand the system in which the actor is enmeshed \citep{granovetter:1973,wellman:1982,wasserman:faust:1994}. Many of the questions that conflict scholars are interested in require a careful application of these lessons. For example, can the actions of a particular actor lead to greater levels of violence between other actors in the system? How does a government crackdown on one group affect the activities of other challenger groups? Grappling with any of these questions requires developing a methodological approach that accounts for dependencies between actors. We accomplish this by introducing a network-based approach that allows us to estimate the interdependencies between actors within an intrastate conflict system even as the composition of actors changes over time. In doing so, our paper is the first to apply an empirical-based network oriented methodology to the study of civil conflict evolution. This framework allows us to estimate the dependencies that manifest between actors in a conflict network and better predict the occurrence of battles between groups within this system. 

To study how violence evolves, we turn to the Nigerian case during 2000-2016. Throughout this time period, Nigeria has seen the rise of multiple armed non-state actor groups. In the southern delta region, armed actors such as the Movement for the Emancipation of the Nigerian Delta (MEND) have battled the government for control of oil production. Boko Haram, potentially the most well known of these groups, has caused massive devastation both to human life and infrastructure in the Northern regions of the country. Accordingly, Boko Haram has challenged the governments' ability to keep order and protect civilians. By using a network approach, we can better understand and predict these dynamics in Nigeria. In particular, this approach yields insights about how the introduction of a violent challenger can alter the trajectory of the conflict. We find that Boko Haram's entrance into the civil war leads to an increase in violence even in unrelated dyads. Excitingly, our approach, which accounts for the networked nature of the conflict in Nigeria, significantly outperforms traditional dyad-group approaches at predicting which groups will have battles, and when.
