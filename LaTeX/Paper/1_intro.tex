\section*{Introduction}

Intrastate conflict is a dynamic, interdependent process that can span regions, involve multiple political actors, and cost thousands of lives. Although the study of intrastate conflict is strongly rooted in a dyadic state-versus-rebels paradigm, scholars are increasingly developing research programs to address the complexities of intrastate conflict \citep{rustad:etal:2011,shortland:etal:2013,nemeth:etal:2014}. One often overlooked feature of intrastate conflict is that it frequently involves more than one influential non-state actor group. This is true whether the conflict manifests as a traditional civil war scenario or as widespread criminal violence with insurgency-like dynamics. For example, the Cuban Revolution, while largely seen as a rebellion led by Fidel Castro and Che Guevara was actually a movement drawn from the strength of multiple non-state actor groups allying against the government. In other regions, like Mexico, we observe drug trafficking organizations (DTOs) not only vying for political influence and territorial control against government actors but also warring against other DTOs.

% 
Sociologists have long argued that to understand an actor's (or group's) behavior it is necessary to understand the system in which the actor operates, and to acknowledge that interactions between actors within that system may be interdependent \citep{granovetter:1973,wellman:1982,wasserman:faust:1994}. Conflict scholars are interested in questions that require applying those lessons. For example, can the actions of a particular actor lead to greater levels of violence between others? How does a government crackdown on an actor affect the activities of other groups in the system? How do actors in the system react if civilians directly protest a particular armed group? Grappling with any of these questions necessitates developing a methodological approach in which we can account for the dependencies between actors. We argue that to understand conflict evolution in contexts where actions between actors are interdependent necessitates a network based approach.

In doing so, our paper is the first to apply a network oriented methodology to the study of civil conflict evolution. We show that our approach more accurately captures the endogenous evolution of civil conflict than alternatives. A network framework for studying conflict evolution is not only more precise--capturing the inherently interdependent structure of conflict--but it enhances our ability to predict how a conflict will develop by taking into account the endogenous structures that develop over time between actors. 

To study how violence evolves, we turn to the Nigerian case from 2000-2016. During these years, Nigeria has seen the rise of many armed non-state actor groups. Boko Haram, potentially the most well known of these groups, formed in 2002 has caused massive devastation both to human life and infrastructure in the Northern regions of the country. This group often targets Christian civilians via the bombing of schools, churches, and other community centers. Accordingly, Boko Haram has deeply challenged the governments' ability to keep order and protect civilians. In addition to this insurgency, the government has faced riots, protests, and other armed militia groups. We suggest this case is useful for analysis due to its multi-party nature and the role that both civilian victimization and civilian mobilization has potentially played in influencing the conflict's development.

The contribution of this study is two-fold. First we explain and demonstrate the importance of considering conflict processes through a network framework. In doing so, we suggest that networks of conflict constitute a meaningful outcome of interest and depart from the great majority of the literature which focuses on dyadic outcomes to measure conflict intensity or duration. Second, we contribute to a growing scholarship concerned with whether or not civilian victimization and mobilization influence trajectories of violence and stability.
