Intrastate conflict is a dynamic, interdependent process that often spans across vast territory, involves multiple political actors, and costs hundreds of thousands of lives. Given this empirical reality, our study presents a novel network analysis of these complexities which accounts for the multidimensional nature of civil conflict. 

Although the study of civil conflict is traditionally rooted in a dyadic state-versus-rebels paradigm, scholars are increasingly motivated to develop research programs to address the dynamic nature of intrastate conflict \citep{rustad:etal:2011,shortland:etal:2013,nemeth:etal:2014}. An often overlooked feature of intrastate conflict is that it frequently involves more than one powerful non-state actor; this is true whether the conflict manifests as a traditional civil war scenario or as widespread criminal violence with insurgency-like dynamics. For example in the current Syrian civil war, Bashar al-Assad's government is involved in conflict with the Islamic State, and multiple militarized opposition groups (the Free Syrian Army, Jaysh al-Islam, Ahrar al-Sham and others): groups that are involved in conflict both with the government, and among themselves. In other regions, like Mexico, we observe drug trafficking organizations (DTOs) who vie both for political influence and territorial control against government actors and other DTOs.

Sociologists have long argued that to understand an actor's (or group's) behavior it is necessary to understand the system in which the actor operates, and to acknowledge that interactions between actors within that system may be interdependent \citep{granovetter:1973,wellman:1982,wasserman:faust:1994}. Many of the questions that conflict scholars are interested in require a careful application of these lessons. For example, can the actions of a particular actor lead to greater levels of violence between other actors in the system? How does a government crackdown on one group affect the activities of other challenger groups?  Grappling with any of these questions requires developing a methodological approach in which we can account for the dependencies between actors. We argue that understanding conflict evolution where actions between actors are interdependent necessitates a network based approach.

In doing so, our paper is the first to apply a network oriented methodology to the study of civil conflict evolution. We show that our approach more accurately captures the endogenous evolution of civil conflict than traditional approaches. A network framework is not only more precise--as it captures the inherently interdependent structure of conflict--but it also enhances our ability to predict how a conflict will develop by taking into account the endogenous structures that develop over time between actors. 

To study how violence evolves, we turn to the Nigerian case from 2000-2016. During these years, Nigeria has seen the rise of multiple armed non-state actor groups. Boko Haram, potentially the most well known of these groups, formed in 2002 has caused massive devastation both to human life and infrastructure in the Northern regions of the country. This group often targets Christian civilians via the bombing of schools, churches, and other community centers. Accordingly, Boko Haram has challenged the governments' ability to keep order and protect civilians. In addition to this insurgency, the government has faced riots, protests, and other armed militia groups. We suggest this case is useful for analysis due to its multi-party nature and the role that both civilian victimization and civilian mobilization has potentially played in shaping the conflict's development.
