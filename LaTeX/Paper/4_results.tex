\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Parameter Estimates}

In examining the results of both a traditional GLM and an AME model, reported in figure \ref{betaEst}, we find interesting substantive results.\footnote{Excluded from these results is the dyadic variable for ``both are government actors." This is included and has a negative effect in both models, but is not substantively interesting.} First notably, we see a non-trivial relationship between the exercise of violence against civilians and participation in a greater number of battles. The effect is larger in the GLM because that model does not account for the differing tendencies for certain groups to engage in different levels of conflict, and likely the groups that commit violence against civilians are generally more conflict prone, but the relationship still holds even in a model with sender and receiver effects. Targeting of civilians is linked both to a higher likelihood of engaging in violence against other military groups, and a higher likelihood of being targeted by other military groups. We cannot conclusively say that these are the results of violence against civilians, but the fact that the relationship holds even in a mixed-effects model (and one that controls for network dependencies) is suggestive.

In the case of this Nigerian conflict network, there is no evidence of a link between protest and participation in battles. There are a number of possible reasons for the null finding here: the protest data we use might have some uncertainty about the targets of the protest,  some protests might agitate towards greater military involvement--``Save Our Girls" protests pushing for the government to confront Boko Haram for example--while other protests might be against violence, or different actors might respond to protest in differing ways. We see no consistent effect of protest, whether we focus only on anti-government protest, only on protests against non-government groups, or when we look at all protests.

Finally, we look at the effect of Boko Haram's entry on the level of conflict in the system and find a clear increase in violence in the years that follow. Again this is not just the effect of Boko Haram's propensity to target and be targeted in battles, as those first order effects are accounted for in the AME model (though that does explain much of the difference between AME and GLM effects). Boko Haram's entry is associated with an increase in conflicts even in the dyads that do not contain Boko Haram. 

\subsection*{Boko Haram's Entrance}

In figures \ref{nigeria_preBK} and \ref{nigeria_postBK}, we show that there is both more intense and more widespread conflict with the entrance of Boko Haram. In the Post Boko Haram network plot, we shade dyadic relationships that saw more conflict after the entrance of Boko Haram in blue, those that saw less in red, and those that saw the same amount in green. Interestingly, much of this conflict does not actually involve Boko Haram. Their conflict is predominantly against the Nigerian Police and Military forces, and yet after their entrance we also see increases in conflict with other groups. 

Our initial intuition was that Boko Haram's conflict with the government weakened the Nigerian state's ability to monopolize violence and deter other violent actors. That the government would be so busy fighting Boko Haram that other militias would be able to run amok. If this were the case, we would expect to see the Police and Military involved in fewer conflicts with other groups in the later period. We do not observe this: not only are the government groups involved in \emph{more} battles on average in the second period, but there level of conflict with nearly every group has increased.

What could be driving these results is that the entrance of Boko Haram has incentivized other militias to ramp up their levels of violence. This could be because they need to increase military strength to defend against Boko Haram, and this leads to security dilemmas and spirals of escalation. Alternatively, it could be because Boko Haram, in searching for a stable base of operations has dislocated groups and strained resources leading to more violence. We need more study and more finely grained data if we want to adjudicate between these, or potential alternative explanations.

What we can say is that the effect of Boko Haram's entrance on conflict demonstrates is the need for a networked approach to interstate conflict. A standard approach to interstate conflict, which focuses on the government-rebel dyads, or in particularly ambitious cases, also looks at rebel-rebel dyads, would catch Boko Haram's direct effect on conflict, but it would miss this indirect effect. We see the entrance of a group to a network increasing the levels of activities in uninvolved dyads. So a traditional dyadic approach would underestimate the effect of Boko Haram, and would underpredict violence in the system.

\subsection*{Out of Sample Performance Analysis}

One of the most important reasons to employ a network model is to help us to better predict and explain conflict. To evaluate our model, we randomly treat approximately 3\% of observations as missing, and estimate the network imputing both the covariates and the presence or absence of a battle -- this is equivalent to a 30-fold cross-validation. We then look at the performance of the model on those deleted observations.

We set a number of benchmarks for comparison. First we compare the AME model to a GLM trained using the same covariates, as discussed above to show the effect of accounting for network dependencies on predicting conflict. We supplement this with an alternative GLM that simply has a lagged dependent variable: the equivalent of saying that conflict and peace are relatively likely to persist between dyads, as well as a GLM with all of our covariates and a lagged dependent variable. 

To evaluate the performance of the models, we rely on three factors: Receiver Operator Characteristic (ROC) curves, Precision Recall (PR) curves, and separation plots. ROC curves look at the tradeoff between true positive rates and false positive rates at different thresholds of classification. An issue with an ROC Curve when looking at conflict, is that it is relatively rare at the dyadic level: in most years only 3\% of possible dyads are in conflict with one another. If peace is common, even a poor model will have a very low False Positive Rate. To get a better sense of which models predict the presence of conflict, not just its absense, we look at Precision Recall Curves. These examine the tradeoffs between the percentage of conflicts a model predicts, and the percentage of predicted conflicts which occur. Lastly, we examine separation plots \citep{greenhill:etal:2011}. These provide an intuitive visualization of the accuracy of our predictions by juxtaposing a line showing the predicted probability of conflict with whether conflict actually occurs for all cases (where the cases are sorted by the predicted probability and then colored to indicate the outcome). Here a perfect model would have all cases where conflict actually exists on the right with a predicted probability of $1$, and would predict $0$ in all other cases. All of the models' performance out of sample by these metrics are displayed in figure \ref{fig:roc_ame}.

The AME model with covariates is the best performing model out of sample in all cases. This model outperforms each of the GLM variants by a large margin.
